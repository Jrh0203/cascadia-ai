Rebuild Cascadia AI as a clean, ambitious v2 repository and continue autonomously until the system is genuinely complete.

MISSION

Create Cascadia AI v2: a fast, maintainable, thoroughly tested, locally trainable, user-friendly Cascadia platform with a genuinely stronger AI. The primary research target is a pre-habitat-bonus mean score of at least 100.0 on the four-player AAAAA ruleset.

Treat every historical research conclusion, benchmark, champion claim, training recipe, failed-experiment note, and architectural assumption in the current repository as untrusted. Do not inherit conclusions merely because they are documented. The existing implementation may be inspected as a reference for rules, UX ideas, test cases, and useful algorithms, but all important claims must be independently reproduced.

Everything must run on the local Apple Silicon cluster consisting of john1, john2, and john3. Do not depend on Modal, cloud GPUs, hosted inference, external training services, or paid compute. Use MLX for all neural-network training and Apple-specific neural computation. Design around the measured CPU, GPU, memory, storage, network, and MLX capabilities of all three machines.

OPERATING STANDARD

This is a permanent rebuild, not an experimental patch series.

- Fix root causes rather than adding compatibility hacks.
- Do not preserve poor abstractions solely for backward compatibility.
- Do not accumulate feature flags, environment-variable experiments, dead strategies, duplicate pipelines, or giant orchestration files.
- Do not leave abandoned code paths in the production tree.
- Keep experimental code isolated from promoted production code.
- Every promoted feature must have tests, documentation, benchmarks, and a clear owner/module.
- Record major architectural decisions in concise ADRs.
- Keep a factual experiment registry containing hypotheses, protocols, results, confidence intervals, artifacts, and conclusions.
- Never promote a model or algorithm from a small or statistically inconclusive benchmark.
- Optimize only after profiling.
- Make experiments reproducible from commands checked into the repository.
- Preserve generated datasets and checkpoints through versioned manifests, not mysterious filenames.
- Build resumable training and evaluation pipelines.
- Do not declare completion while required tests, documentation, UX, reproducibility, or benchmark validation remain unfinished.

DISTRIBUTED LOCAL COMPUTE

john1 is the coordinator and primary development machine. john2 and john3 are authorized local compute workers reachable through the existing `john2` and `john3` SSH aliases over Tailscale. Full permission is granted to SSH into both workers, provision the repository and required local dependencies, start and monitor compute jobs, retrieve artifacts, and stop or restart jobs when needed for Cascadia research.

Proactively distribute meaningful parallel workloads across john1, john2, and john3. This is the default expectation for data collection, simulation, benchmarks, parameter sweeps, independent training runs, ablations, and other divisible experiments. Do not leave john2 and john3 idle while a long-running workload could safely benefit from them. Tiny smoke tests, latency-sensitive debugging, and inherently serial tasks may remain on john1.

Distributed work must be rigorous:

- Verify node health, available disk, memory pressure, power settings, and current workloads before dispatch.
- Synchronize the exact source revision, configuration, dataset schema, model inputs, and dependency environment used by every worker.
- Record the hostname, hardware, source revision, command, seed range, start time, runtime, and output manifest for each shard.
- Assign deterministic, disjoint seed or game-index ranges. Never duplicate samples accidentally or mix train, validation, and held-out test partitions.
- Write independent resumable shards with checksums and atomic completion markers; never have multiple workers append unsafely to one file.
- Aggregate and validate worker artifacts on john1 before training, analysis, promotion, or deletion.
- Detect failed, partial, stale, or duplicated shards and rerun only the missing work.
- Keep benchmark comparisons statistically valid when work is split across machines, including paired seeds where required.
- Use MLX locally on each Apple Silicon node where appropriate. Do not pretend independent processes are synchronized multi-node training unless a real, tested distributed algorithm implements that behavior.
- Avoid oversubscription. Preserve enough headroom for SSH, telemetry, artifact transfer, and the interactive dashboard.
- Prefer native macOS workers unless containers solve a demonstrated reproducibility problem without sacrificing Apple Silicon or MLX performance.
- Keep the cluster dashboard accurate enough to show reachability, utilization, health, and active Cascadia workloads.

PRODUCT REQUIREMENTS

1. A Correct, Fast Rules Engine

- Exact Cascadia rules for 1-4 players.
- AAAAA as the primary research configuration.
- Habitat bonuses configurable and excluded from the primary 100-point metric.
- All wildlife scoring variants supported if they can be verified.
- Correct market overflow replacement, mulligans, independent drafting, nature tokens, bag behavior, rotations, and habitat connectivity.
- Deterministic seeded simulation.
- Efficient apply/undo or immutable transitions suitable for search.
- Stable serialization and hashing.
- Property tests, rule fixtures, regression tests, and scoring tests.
- Clear separation between rules, simulation, search, models, training, evaluation, and presentation.

2. A Clean AI Platform

- A small, coherent set of production strategies.
- A clearly separated experimental research layer.
- Typed configuration instead of scattered environment-variable behavior.
- One canonical move representation and legal-move generator.
- One canonical benchmark protocol.
- Profiling and benchmark tools that measure move generation, scoring, inference, search, and complete games.
- Reproducible seeded comparisons with paired trials and confidence intervals.
- No reliance on historical weight files as the final solution.

3. MLX-Native Neural Research

- Use MLX for neural training and Apple GPU computation.
- Build a clean dataset format with schema versions, metadata, validation, checksums, and streaming support.
- Implement resumable collection and training.
- Support train/validation/test seed partitions to prevent benchmark leakage.
- Investigate architectures from first principles, including:
  - compact spatial hex networks,
  - residual HexCNNs,
  - entity/set encoders for market, bags, opponents, and habitat races,
  - policy and decomposed value heads,
  - distributional or uncertainty-aware values where justified,
  - teacher distillation,
  - self-play and search-guided policy iteration.
- Choose architectures through measured accuracy, ranking quality, inference latency, calibration, and playing strength.
- Keep Rust and MLX boundaries explicit and well documented.
- Avoid redundant Rust implementations of neural training.
- Provide one-command local training, resumption, evaluation, and checkpoint promotion.

4. Research From Scratch

Build a fresh experimental program rather than replaying old assumptions.

Start with independently measured baselines:

- random,
- simple legal greedy,
- pattern-aware heuristic,
- pure learned policy/value,
- search-enhanced learned model.

Then investigate where score is lost:

- candidate-generation recall,
- market drafting,
- wildlife allocation,
- habitat construction,
- nature-token value,
- multi-turn pattern commitments,
- opponent competition,
- stochastic rollout variance,
- value error by phase and scoring category,
- theoretical or empirical upper bounds.

For every experiment:

- state the hypothesis,
- define the exact protocol before running,
- use held-out seeds,
- record runtime and hardware,
- report sample count, mean, variance, confidence interval, and category breakdown,
- compare against a fixed baseline,
- reject inconclusive claims,
- preserve enough information to reproduce the result.

Do not assume MCE, NNUE, MCTS, AlphaZero, distillation, or any historical approach is correct. Test them fairly. Search the relevant primary literature when choosing established algorithms, but let local evidence determine promotion.

5. Performance

- Profile before rewriting hot paths.
- Make full-game simulation fast enough for meaningful local experimentation.
- Batch MLX inference where beneficial.
- Minimize Rust/Python boundary overhead.
- Build deterministic performance benchmarks.
- Provide interactive strength levels with explicit latency budgets.
- Aim for:
  - instant or near-instant basic suggestions,
  - strong interactive moves in a few seconds,
  - an unrestricted research mode for maximum strength,
  - overnight local training that checkpoints and resumes safely.
- Document measured throughput on this machine.

PRIMARY SUCCESS METRIC

AAAAA, four-player symmetric play, base score excluding habitat bonuses:

- Development comparisons must use paired deterministic seed suites.
- Final validation must use at least 1,000 held-out games or 4,000 independently scored seat-games.
- Report mean, standard deviation, standard error, 95% confidence interval, percentiles, and score breakdown.
- The target is mean base score >= 100.0.
- A 100-point claim is only valid if it survives the held-out benchmark and is not caused by scoring, opponent, seat, seed, or bonus inflation.
- Also compare head-to-head performance against the strongest independently reproduced v1 baseline.
